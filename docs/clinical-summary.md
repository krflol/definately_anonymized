# Clinical Summary

## Current Status

An adult male in his 30s has a patient-right cervical lymph-node conglomerate / neck mass. Core needle biopsy of the node showed metastatic squamous cell carcinoma, moderate to poorly differentiated.

Key pathology features:

- CK5 positive.
- p16 positive with block staining.
- Flow cytometry: no significant lymphoid immunophenotypic abnormalities.

The current working frame is metastatic squamous cell carcinoma in a right cervical lymph node, with HPV-mediated oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma as a leading possibility.

Initial PET/CT staging now supports a provisional `M0` / local-regional frame: the report identified avid right neck nodal disease and tonsillar/oropharyngeal uptake requiring clinical correlation, but did not report suspicious chest, abdomen, pelvis, or bone uptake. The exact TNM notation and stage grouping still need confirmation because HPV-mediated oropharyngeal staging differs from conventional/p16-negative staging.

On `Day +29`, a diagnostic primary-site procedure inspected the oral cavity, oropharynx, larynx, and hypopharynx; obtained small patient-right base-of-tongue biopsies; and removed the patient-right tonsil en bloc. No neck dissection was performed. Tonsil and base-of-tongue pathology remain pending, and the known cervical-node cancer remains known disease.

## Plain-Language Meaning

The biopsy confirmed squamous cell carcinoma in a right neck lymph node. Because it is in a lymph node, the word `metastatic` means it likely spread from a primary tumor somewhere else. It does not, by itself, prove distant metastatic disease.

p16 block positivity strongly raises the possibility of HPV-mediated disease, commonly from the oropharynx, such as tonsil or base of tongue. It does not by itself identify the primary site or prove HPV16 genotype.

The flow cytometry result makes lymphoma less likely as the explanation for the biopsied node.

The recent operation does not yet establish that the cancer source was removed. A positive tonsil with negative tongue-base biopsies would support a tonsil primary; a positive tongue-base biopsy would identify a site that was sampled but not resected. If both are negative, the primary remains occult or unsampled. The node diagnosis remains valid in all outcomes.
