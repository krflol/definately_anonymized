# Diagnostic Primary-Site Procedure

This is an anonymized summary of the `Day +29` operative event. It is a clinical abstraction, not copied medical-record text.

## Documented Purpose

The procedure was performed to identify the primary site responsible for the biopsy-proven p16-positive squamous cell carcinoma in a patient-right cervical lymph node.

The operative purpose was diagnostic primary-site localization. The record did not describe complete removal of all known cancer.

## Procedures And Findings

- Suspension microlaryngoscopy was used to inspect the oral cavity, oropharynx, larynx, and hypopharynx.
- A few small biopsies were taken from the patient-right base of tongue for permanent pathology.
- The patient-right tonsil was dissected just outside its capsule, removed en bloc, and sent for permanent pathology.
- No cervical lymph-node excision or neck dissection was described.
- No visible primary site or separately submitted tumor-bed margins were documented in the operative summary.
- Blood loss was minimal, no complications were reported, and recovery transfer was stable.

## What This Does And Does Not Establish

This is meaningful progress because it supplied tissue from two plausible oropharyngeal primary sites.

It does not yet prove that the primary cancer was removed:

| Tonsil pathology | Base-of-tongue pathology | Working interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Carcinoma present | No carcinoma identified | The patient-right tonsil is the likely primary. Gross primary-site tissue may have been removed, but pathology and the surgeon must determine whether margins and procedure intent provide definitive control. |
| No carcinoma identified | Carcinoma present | The patient-right base of tongue is the likely primary. Only small biopsies were taken, so that primary remains in place. |
| Carcinoma present | Carcinoma present | Specialist pathology and tumor-board interpretation are needed to define site, extent, and whether one or more processes are present. |
| No carcinoma identified | No carcinoma identified | The primary remains occult or unsampled. The established patient-right cervical-node SCC diagnosis remains valid. |

The known cervical-node cancer remains present in every branch because no neck dissection was performed.

## Staging Consequences

HPV-associated oropharyngeal cancers diagnosed in 2026 are staged with AJCC Version 9. Earlier AJCC 8 estimates in this record are historical approximations, not the current formal stage.

Because no neck dissection occurred, this operation cannot provide a complete pathologic nodal category, involved-node count, or pathologic extranodal-extension assessment. Request a written clinical line such as:

> `cT__ cN__ cM0, AJCC Version 9 HPV-associated oropharynx, clinical Stage __; imaging-detected ENE present/absent/indeterminate.`

If tonsil carcinoma is found, the specimen may support a pathologic T category and margin assessment, but `pN` may remain unassigned unless adequate nodal surgery occurs.

## Treatment And Trial Questions

1. Which specimen identifies the primary site?
2. If the tonsil is positive, was the resection adequate for definitive primary-site control?
3. What is the plan for the known patient-right cervical-node disease?
4. Is another operation planned, and would it be the definitive oncologic surgery?
5. What postoperative or definitive radiation/chemoradiation is recommended after final pathology?
6. Does the anatomy permit unilateral treatment, or is bilateral treatment required?
7. Should HPV ctDNA be drawn before definitive nodal treatment, with the timing labeled accurately?

The public criteria for `NCT06223568` require newly diagnosed, surgically resectable Stage I-II HPV-positive OPSCC, planned standard cancer-removal surgery, and a tumor site amenable to pretreatment biopsy. They do not explicitly answer whether a prior diagnostic tonsillectomy is disqualifying. Eligibility is therefore uncertain and requires direct trial-site review after pathology and the definitive surgical plan are known. Standard treatment should not be delayed for screening.

FDA-approved perioperative pembrolizumab must begin before definitive surgery in the specified PD-L1-positive, resectable locally advanced setting. This diagnostic procedure alone does not prove that the window has passed, but applicability remains a tumor-board question rather than an expected treatment.

## Immediate Recovery Guardrails

The surgeon's discharge plan controls over this general summary.

- Contact the surgical team for any fresh oral or throat bleeding.
- Active or repeated bright-red bleeding, clots, repeated spitting or vomiting blood, breathing difficulty, faintness, or bleeding that does not promptly stop requires emergency evaluation.
- Inability to maintain fluids, markedly reduced or dark urine, pain uncontrolled by the prescribed plan, fever at the discharge-instruction threshold, or rapidly worsening swelling requires same-day clinical contact.
- Do not add aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, anticoagulants, or supplements that may affect bleeding unless the surgeon's plan permits them. Tonsillectomy NSAID instructions vary.
- If a prescribed pain medicine contains acetaminophen, include it when calculating any additional acetaminophen and confirm the daily limit with the surgeon or pharmacist.
- Prioritize frequent fluids and tolerated calorie/protein intake. Avoid smoking/vaping, alcohol, heavy exertion, and straining for the period specified by the surgeon.
